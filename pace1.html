PACE 1.0 Accelerator Chip

Silicon-Proven CGRA Technology Achieving 360 GOPS/W

Successfully Taped Out & Tested

Achievement Milestone

The PACE 1.0 chip was successfully sent for fabrication in December 2023 and has been thoroughly tested and measured. Fabricated in UMC 40nm CMOS technology, the accelerator features an 8×8 matrix of 64 processing elements (PEs) with 8 data memory modules implemented using dual-port RAMs, achieving exceptional energy efficiency for edge computing workloads.

The PACE CGRA can run at a maximum frequency of 105 MHz while consuming only 43mW at 1V supply (100% PE utilization), achieving a maximum energy efficiency of 360 GOPS/W at 0.6V and 21MHz. This represents the successful achievement of our goal to deliver 500 GOPS/W peak performance with 1hr battery life at weight of 1kg by the mid-term mark of the project.

Process-Normalized Performance

  • Currently, our design achieved simulated peak energy efficiency of 142 GOPS/W, 462 GOPS/W, and 568 GOPS/W at 0.9V, 0.5V, and 0.45V, respectively
  • When process normalization is considered against state-of-the-art accelerators from KU Leuven (using 22nm FDX and 28nm CMOS), PACE 1.0 offers a peak energy efficiency of 673 GOPS/W and ~1090 GOPS/W when normalized to 28nm and 22nm processes, respectively
  • This demonstrates highly competitive performance compared to ISSCC'19 and SSCL'20 from KU Leuven when accounting for process technology differences

PACE Architecture: 8×8 PE Array with Memory Modules and Instruction Set

Architecture Overview

The block diagram of PACE accelerator consists of an 8×8 matrix of computation cores (PEs) in the center and 8 data memory modules on the left and right sides of the PE matrix, implemented using dual-ports RAMs. Each PE supports 17 instructions which are co-optimized and selected via analyzing the major kernels required for edge computing workloads.

Processing Element Design

  • Configuration Memory: For dynamically setting the behavior and connectivity of the tile (0.25KB per PE)
  • Arithmetic Logic Unit (ALU): Performs logic computation such as add, shift, and multiplication with 17 optimized operations
  • Input Registers & Crossbar: R0-R4 input registers and input/output crossbar to handle data flow between different tiles
  • Energy Efficiency Focus: With improved ALU and memory integration, PE power consumption is reduced up to 19.06% to ~700 µW at 0.9V, 100 MHz
PEs: 64 (8×8 array)
Instructions: 17 per PE
Data Memory: 8 × 8KB dual-port
Config Memory: 0.25KB per PE

Power Optimization Techniques

CGRA workload differs depending on applications and kernels. We explore this characteristic and embed No-Operation (NOP) instruction with hardware infrastructure so that individual tiles can be clock gated by the compiler. With the NOP and clock-gating optimization, the system power consumption in idle mode is reduced by 24%.

NOP State Microarchitecture

  • Compiler-Driven Clock Gating: Individual tiles can be selectively clock gated when executing NOP instructions, significantly reducing idle power
  • Logic-Only to Gated Clock: The implementation includes NOP decode logic, clock gate with enable signal, and PM logic with counter for cycle control
  • ALU Operation Power: Different instructions consume varying power - multiplication (123 µW) is highest, while comparison operations (34-42 µW) are most efficient at 100MHz, 0.9V
Area & Power Breakdown: In the RISC-V PACE SoC, PACE takes ~30% of die (4mm × 2.4mm). Within PACE, 64 PEs take 71% of area. During processing (microspeech kernel), PEs consume 97% of PACE power, with configuration memory dominating at 52% of PE power.

PACE-V SoC with Rocket CPU Integration and Peripherals

RISC-V Based SoC

Although our focus is to develop highly energy-efficient PACE accelerator, our analysis showed it is extremely inefficient to operate PACE as a stand-alone chip with an external microcontroller. Not only does this greatly reduce data transfer bandwidth (and thus performance), but it also incurs additional power consumption due to chip-to-chip data transfer.

Thus, we decided to integrate PACE together with a RISC-V processor (Rocket CPU) in SoC. Within this SoC chip, PACE accelerator works as one peripheral of the RISC-V core, enabling high-bandwidth on-chip communication and eliminating external data transfer overhead.

Rocket CPU Core Features

  • 5-stage in-order, scalar core implementing RV32G/RV64G ISAs with MMU supporting page-based virtual memory
  • Non-blocking data cache and front-end with branch prediction (BTB, BHT, RAS)
  • Floating-point support: Berkeley's Chisel implementations of FP units
  • Privilege levels: RISC-V machine, supervisor, and user privilege levels
  • Extensively validated: Multiple functional silicon prototypes have been manufactured
BootROM Firmware: Fully written in RISC-V assembly (~200 bytes), fetches executable from external I2C EEPROM chip, includes 50ms bootup delay for system stability, with hardcoded settings for 8KB memory size, 400KHz speed, and vendor ID/address

Clock Root for RISC-V PACE-SoC

For PACE v1.0 tape out, a clock root is implemented to supply clock source to the entire design. Clock signals are provided to both the RISC-V based microcontroller and the PACE CGRA. The maximum frequency is 400MHz. Internally, 3 levels of division (divide-by-2, 4, and 8) are used to reduce clock frequency down to 50MHz.

Clock Distribution

  • clk_ref (50 MHz): Reference clock for PLL
  • clk_pll (400 MHz): PLL-HiP output clock for internal system
  • clk_div* (50-400 MHz): Clock divider array with configurable division
  • SDRAM clocks: Dedicated clocks for SDRAM with configurable delay
  • SD card & Debug: Separate clocks for SD card and JTAG debug interfaces

Post-layout simulation was performed to verify the outputs of the clock root, ensuring proper timing and signal integrity across all clock domains in the SoC.

  • Router: 14% of PE power
  • ALU: 8% of PE power

FPGA Emulation Platform

We migrated the PACE RTL design to the Digilent Genesys 2 FPGA (Xilinx Kintex-7™ FPGA XC7K325T-2FFG900C) board for verification and application demonstration purposes. With the clock generator being mapped to MMCM module and SRAM being mapped to the block RAM module, the emulation of the PACE design is successful on the evaluation board.

Verification & Development

  • Successfully Verified Applications: As of March 2023, we have verified two applications (Array Add and MicroSpeech) running on FPGA-based PACE design
  • Dual Deployment: Two FPGA boards (one in IME and one in NUS) are currently deployed to enable our team members to develop different applications on this platform
  • MMCM Mapping: Clock generator mapped to FPGA's Mixed-Mode Clock Manager for precise frequency control
  • Block RAM Utilization: SRAM efficiently mapped to FPGA's block RAM resources for optimal performance
Development Platform: The FPGA emulation enables rapid prototyping and testing of applications before chip fabrication, supporting the complete development workflow from algorithm design to hardware validation

WalkingWizard EEG System in Use with PACE Integration

Real-World Application: WalkingWizard

We have leveraged the PACE 1.0 accelerator chip to enhance the performance of EEG devices. The PACE 1.0 chip aligns well with the demands of wearable EEG systems, providing the necessary computational power while maintaining portability and energy efficiency.

WalkingWizard, a truly wearable EEG headset designed for everyday use, integrates dry electrodes with flexible PCB assembly to achieve accurate EEG sensing even under motion. By incorporating the PACE 1.0 chip, we aim to improve the real-time processing capabilities of WalkingWizard, enhancing its performance in capturing and analyzing EEG signals during various activities such as walking or engaging in VR environments.

Integration Benefits

  • Real-Time Processing: On-device signal processing for emotion monitoring and cognitive state assessment
  • Mobile Brain-Computer Interfaces: Advanced applications for VR interaction and daily activity monitoring
  • Energy Efficiency: Extended battery life through PACE's ultra-low power consumption
  • System Integration: Mapped EEG application on PACE 1.0 and designed new PCB system to integrate the chip
